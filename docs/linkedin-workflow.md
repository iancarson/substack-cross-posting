# LinkedIn Cross-Posting Workflow for Substack Writers

A complete guide to turning Substack Notes and articles into high-performing LinkedIn posts that drive newsletter subscribers.

---

## Why LinkedIn Is Your Best External Growth Channel

LinkedIn is the highest-converting external platform for most newsletter writers:

- **Professional intent:** LinkedIn users actively seek expertise and insight — exactly what newsletters provide
- **Organic reach:** LinkedIn's algorithm still rewards native content with significant unpaid distribution
- **Subscribe conversion:** LinkedIn readers who click through to Substack convert at 2–5% (higher than most other channels)
- **No follower requirement:** A well-written post can reach thousands even with a small following

## The LinkedIn Cross-Posting Rules

### Rule 1: No links in the post body

LinkedIn's algorithm suppresses posts that contain external links in the body text. This is well-documented and consistent across account sizes.

**Instead:** Post your content with no links, then immediately add your Substack subscribe link as the first comment.

**Example workflow:**
1. Publish LinkedIn post (no link in body)
2. Within 30 seconds: add comment with "Full newsletter + subscribe link: [your Substack URL]"

### Rule 2: First 150 characters are the only characters that matter initially

LinkedIn shows roughly 150 characters before the "see more" break. If your first sentence doesn't compel the click, most readers won't read the rest.

**Test:** Paste your LinkedIn post's first sentence into a tweet. If it works as a tweet, it'll work as a LinkedIn hook.

### Rule 3: Native content, not Substack summaries

Don't write LinkedIn posts that say "Here's what I covered in this week's newsletter." Write LinkedIn posts that stand alone as complete insights — and mention the newsletter only at the end.

A reader who gets value from the LinkedIn post will subscribe. A reader who gets a summary will feel satisfied without subscribing.

## The 4 LinkedIn Post Formats That Work for Newsletter Writers

### Format 1: The Insight Post

**Best for:** Sharing a core perspective from your newsletter topic
**Length:** 300–800 characters

Structure:
1. Counter-intuitive opening claim (1 sentence)
2. 3–4 supporting points or evidence (bullets or short paragraphs)
3. Synthesis (1 sentence)
4. CTA ("I write about this weekly — subscribe link in comments")

### Format 2: The List Post

**Best for:** Tactical, actionable content
**Length:** 400–900 characters

Structure:
1. Opening frame ("5 things most [audience] get wrong about [topic]:")
2. Numbered list (4–7 items, 1–2 sentences each)
3. Short closing CTA

### Format 3: The Story Post

**Best for:** Building audience trust and connection
**Length:** 500–1,200 characters

Structure:
1. Set the scene (specific moment or experience)
2. What happened
3. What you learned
4. How it applies to the reader
5. CTA

### Format 4: The Question Post

**Best for:** Driving comments (which boost algorithmic reach)
**Length:** 200–400 characters

Structure:
1. 1–2 sentence context/setup
2. One specific, answerable question
3. Optional: your own tentative answer

## Adapting Substack Notes for LinkedIn

Most Substack Notes need two changes to work on LinkedIn:

**Change 1: Rewrite the opening line**

Substack notes can assume the reader is in your world. LinkedIn readers aren't. Make the opening line completely standalone and professional.

Before (Substack): *"The thing everyone gets wrong about this..."*
After (LinkedIn): *"Most newsletter writers plateau at 500 subscribers and think it's a content quality problem. It's almost never a content quality problem."*

**Change 2: Remove Substack-specific references**

Replace: "restack," "Notes feed," "Substack recommendations"
With: "share," "social media," "newsletter cross-promotion"

Everything else — the insight, the structure, the evidence — stays the same.

## Publishing Schedule

**Post 3x per week** for sustained LinkedIn growth without diminishing returns.

Suggested schedule:
- **Monday:** Insight post (sets the week's intellectual tone)
- **Wednesday:** List or tactical post (highest engagement, midweek)
- **Friday:** Story or question post (drives weekend conversation)
